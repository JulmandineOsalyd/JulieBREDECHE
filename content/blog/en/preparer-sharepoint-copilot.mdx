Everyone is talking about it right now — structuring content is not an optional step in a Copilot deployment.
It is the condition for Copilot to be reliable.

The problem is that asking users to "properly tag their content" always remains complicated. The tips I share here rely on a simple principle: make tagging so fast and so visual that users do it naturally.

Here are two concrete tips I apply systematically whenever I create a collaborative space for a team or a department.

---

## Why It Starts in SharePoint

Copilot exploits data where it lives. For most organizations, that's Teams or SharePoint.

Two problems come up repeatedly in the field:

1. **Permissions are a mess.** Documents shared ad hoc, forgotten unique permissions — nobody really knows who sees what. Copilot respects access rights and can surface information that is supposed to be confidential.

2. **Everything is equal.** A 2019 meeting note carries the same weight as a current HR procedure. Copilot doesn't know what's important if you don't signal it.

Both problems have a simple solution: well-designed columns, JSON-formatted, that users can activate in one click.

---

## Tip 1: Control Sharing with Dedicated Columns

In SharePoint, every time a user shares a document directly with someone (a "Specific people" link), they step outside the library's initial permissions scheme.

My solution:

- **A "Sharing Status" column**: when a user manually shares a document, they click to flag it.
The column changes appearance: a colored dot clearly indicates that this document has specific permissions.

- **A "Stop Sharing" button**: one click triggers a Power Automate flow that restores permissions inheritance and resets the "Sharing Status" column value. The document returns to the parent library's permissions. No more orphaned permissions.

> What this changes for Copilot: agents operate within a **clean, controlled permissions scope**. The risk of information leaking to users who shouldn't have access is greatly reduced.

---

## Tip 2: Identify Key Knowledge with a Toggle

Not all documents are equal. But how do you distinguish a reference procedure from a working draft without complicating users' lives?

My solution: **a Yes/No column** titled "Key Knowledge", JSON-formatted as a visual toggle.

One click is all it takes to flip the toggle. The document is immediately identified as a priority knowledge source.

Today, I use this column to surface these documents in the **SharePoint search engine**.

---

## What This Changes in Practice

These tips are very easy to implement and integrate into users' daily workflow without friction.

Their impact on Copilot result quality is immediate:

- Sharing is more readable day-to-day and easy to manage
- Important content is progressively identified

This is also what preparing SharePoint for AI looks like: simple choices, embedded in users' daily routines, that make information exploitable.
