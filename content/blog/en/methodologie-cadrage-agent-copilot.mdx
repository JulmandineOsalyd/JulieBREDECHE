> *"We'd like a Copilot agent for our HR department. Can you build it for us?"*

The request comes in regularly, framed like a classic dev project.

Except that building a **declarative Copilot agent** (the kind you create inside Copilot Chat, without Copilot Studio) isn't development — it's business scoping. Flip those proportions, and you end up with an agent that works technically but no one uses after three weeks.

Here's my 5-phase method.

---

## Phase 1 • Identify prerequisites and stakeholders

A scoping call with the **business requesters**.

My standard questions:

- **If the agent could only handle 3 priority use cases, which ones?** The question forces the business to make trade-offs. They usually show up with a list of 15 ideas. We leave with 3.
- **Who will use the agent?** Employees, managers, HR team? And more importantly, why?
- **Which Copilot licenses do you have?** I ask them to get the info from IT, or connect me directly if needed.

> **Without the right licenses in place, the agent simply cannot be used.**

By the end of this call, the business sponsor and the IT reference are identified. If either is missing, we don't move to phase 2.

---

## Phase 2 • Structure the knowledge and prepare the content

The quality of a declarative agent depends 90% on the content you give it to read.

Two workshops:

- **Help defining use cases** with the business: we list recurring questions, time-consuming processes, pain points.
- **Data governance workshop with IT**: which sources can we connect? Which data is sensitive?

I can then propose a clear methodology to guide the business through selecting knowledge sources and structuring the content.

---

## Phase 3 • Select and document the target process

This is where we really define the agent's behavior.

At a minimum, I run the following workshops:

1. **Use case / knowledge mapping**: for every question the agent will handle, we identify the associated source of truth.
2. **Data and knowledge sources**: we validate the SharePoint sources or other files that will feed the agent.
3. **Tone, stance, and limits** of the agent: formal or informal address, level of detail, behavior when a question is out of scope.
4. **Confidentiality and legal framework**: sensitive data to exclude, disclaimers to display, GDPR compliance.

> **Deliverable**: an **agent specification** document. It becomes the reference for every iteration that follows.

---

## Phase 4 • Configure the agent over several iterations

One iteration per week keeps the project on track and forces concrete delivery each cycle.

For the first three iterations, the **requesters** (the project team) do the testing. They know the need and can spot what's off. A broader pilot group steps in during the last iteration.

**Iteration 1: First working version of the agent**

I plug in the main sources, write a first instruction prompt, and set the baseline behavior. The agent responds to the priority use cases identified in phase 1.

The project team tests the priority use cases and gives concrete feedback on what works and what doesn't.

**Iteration 2: Adjusting sources and prompts**

Most of the refinement work happens here. We remove documents that pollute the answers, add the ones that were missing, and restructure the knowledge base if needed.

Instructions are tweaked to fix the drifts noticed during early testing.

**Iteration 3: Refining tone, limits, and trick questions**

We work on the agent's posture: formal or informal address, answer length, direct or more educational tone. And most importantly, we test **trick questions**:

- **Out-of-scope questions**: an employee asking an HR agent for IT info.
- **Ambiguous questions**: a wording that could apply to two different topics.

**Iteration 4: Pilot rollout**

A small group of 5-6 end users (outside the project team) tests the agent in real conditions during the week.

It's the first time the agent meets users who don't know the project. The feedback is often very different from what the project team gave.

---

## Phase 5 • Deploy and share the agent with end users

In most cases, this is an IT step. They have the admin rights and they know the organization's distribution channels.

Three sharing options depending on context:

- **Direct Copilot link**: the fastest — send a link to targeted users. Works for small audiences.
- **Published on a SharePoint site**: the agent becomes accessible to authorized users. Ideal when the target population is large.
- **Integration into a dedicated Teams app**: for agents that fit into daily usage.

> **If users don't have access to the agent's knowledge sources, it will quickly become unusable.**

## My takeaway

Building a successful declarative Copilot agent means accepting that configuration isn't the heart of the project.

Three things I see over and over:

1. **Projects that fail** almost always come down to incomplete business scoping, an IT team that wasn't involved enough, or poorly managed governance.
2. A **declarative agent's quality depends 90% on its sources**, not its configuration.
3. **Without a business sponsor, the agent won't live long.** Even with the best specifications in the world.
