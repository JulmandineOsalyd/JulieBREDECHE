Automating a process isn't just stacking up a series of actions and conditions. Here are the 5 mistakes I see most often during my engagements.

---

## Mistake #1: Automating a broken process

A broken process automated isn't a fixed process. It's bugs that hit faster, at a bigger scale.

Before opening Power Automate, three reflexes:

- **Analyze** the existing process: what are the key steps? What are the exceptions?
- **Clarify the roles**: who does what, and when? Who approves? Who gets notified?
- **Simplify**: are all the steps really necessary? Some may just exist out of habit.

> **Automation doesn't fix a process. It amplifies its flaws.**

---

## Mistake #2: Trying to automate everything

Early enthusiasm pushes teams to automate broadly. Wrong reflex. Not every task deserves it.

| Good candidate for automation | Better kept human |
|---|---|
| ✅ &nbsp; Simple and repetitive task | ❌ &nbsp; Many case-by-case exceptions |
| ✅ &nbsp; Clear rules, little ambiguity | ❌ &nbsp; Sensitive or subjective decision |
| ✅ &nbsp; No human judgment required | ❌ &nbsp; Context changes constantly |

A task full of exceptions, automated, means as many conditional branches in the flow. A nightmare to maintain, an unreadable logic six months later.

> **Automate, yes. Automate everything, no.**

---

## Mistake #3: Neglecting adoption

A flow that works technically but no one uses is a useless flow. I've seen brilliant automations die because no one took the time to explain what they actually changed in people's daily work.

Three levers to activate from day one:

- **Involve** future users: not just sponsors, the actual end users. Right from the scoping phase.
- **Explain**: what's it for? What problem does it solve for them? What time is saved?
- **Train**: short demos, available support, accessible FAQ. Not just an email with a link.

> **An unadopted automation is a failure, even if it runs perfectly.**

---

## Mistake #4: Forgetting the exceptions

The flow handles the nominal case well. And the day the approver is on leave? The day the source file is empty? The day the third-party API returns an error?

An unhandled edge case doesn't save time. It becomes a blocker that no one knows how to solve.

What to plan for from the design phase:

- **Alternative scenarios**: missing actor, missing data, unexpected value
- **Alerts and escalations**: a human gets notified when the flow can't decide
- **Controls**: upstream checks to guarantee reliability, not just execution

> **A good flow doesn't just work. It knows how to say it doesn't know.**

---

## Mistake #5: Believing that delivered = finished

Going live isn't the end of the project. It's the start of its real life. A flow that gets abandoned after deployment ages badly: new use cases, tools evolving, user feedback ignored.

A system needs to be steered. Concretely:

- **Measure results**: tracking indicators, run monitoring, failure rates
- **Adjust**: integrate field feedback and use cases that weren't anticipated at the start
- **Aim for agility**: a good tool evolves with the organization and the platform versions

> **A system you abandon decays. A system you steer gets better.**

---

## My takeaway

Automating a process effectively is, in my view, mostly a matter of methodology.

Three observations that come back in most projects:

1. **Success is decided by clean scoping**, a simplified process, and clear roles.
2. **Adoption is half the project.** An automation that works technically but no one uses has no business value.
3. **A flow must evolve over time**, in line with new business constraints or organizational changes.

Listen, adjust, keep it simple.
