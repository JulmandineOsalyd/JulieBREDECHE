Since Copilot arrived in Power Automate, I hear the same question often:

**"With Copilot, do we still need an expert to create Power Automate flows?"**

I understand the question: Copilot generates flows in natural language. It proposes actions, structures conditions, suggests connectors. It's impressive.

But building a flow is often the easiest part.

---

## What Copilot does very well in Power Automate

Copilot is an excellent tool for getting started with Power Automate:

- It generates a **first draft** of a flow in seconds, handy when you're new and don't know Power Automate actions.
- It helps **find the syntax** for certain expressions
- It **explains an error** without needing to go through each action

But for now, Copilot is still not very good at configuring each action and often generates errors that make flows unusable.

> **The real challenge of an automation project isn't technical, it's understanding the context.**

---

## What Copilot can't do

### 1. Understand what the client really wants

When a client tells me *"we want to automate our purchase order entry"*, that's not a flow description. **It's the starting point of a requirements statement**.

What you need to untangle next:

- Who approves? In what order? What if the approver is absent?
- What does a "purchase order" mean for them? An email? An Excel file? A scanned paper form?
- Which systems need to be updated in parallel?
- What happens if the order is rejected at step 3 out of 4?

These questions, Copilot doesn't ask. It takes your prompt literally and builds something that seems to work, **but doesn't match the client's reality**.

---

### 2. Navigate the constraints of the environment

Power Automate is a tool in a complex Microsoft ecosystem, which has its rules and limits.

| Constraint | What AI ignores |
|---|---|
| **Licenses** | Which premium connector is used? Who has a premium Power Automate license? |
| **Governance** | Do DLP policies allow this connector in that environment? |
| **Platform limits** | How many actions per flow? What data volume is expected? |
| **Connectors** | Is the Power Automate version enough, or do you need the third-party connector? |
| **Environments** | Dev, test, prod: how do you manage connections and variables between environments? |

A flow generated by Copilot can be technically correct and completely unusable depending on the governance established at the client.

---

### 3. Anticipate exceptions and manage errors

Years of experience can't be replaced. After years of projects, I've built a mental catalog of "classic mistakes":

- Flows that fail silently without error notification
- Conditions that don't account for exceptions
- Automations that work in test and fail in production due to volume
- Approval timeout periods not anticipated

---

### 4. Support change management

A deployed flow that nobody uses is a failed project.

The technical part is only half the work. The other half is:

- Explaining to colleagues **why** their process is changing
- Training functional administrators who will maintain the solution
- Managing resistance (*"before we did it this way and it worked great"*)
- Adapting the solution when field feedback reveals unarticulated needs

---

## My conclusion

I think the real question isn't "will AI replace experts?" but "**what has real value in an expert's work?**"

Placing a "Send an email" action after a "Create a SharePoint item" action remains relatively accessible. What has real added value is:

✅ Translating a vague business need into a robust solution<br/>
✅ Knowing Microsoft's limits to build a viable long-term project<br/>
✅ Understanding licensing to control project costs<br/>
✅ Supporting teams through change<br/>

**Copilot can help go faster on certain technical aspects, but it doesn't replace what really matters: understanding the business, scoping the need, delivering a solution that works well in a specific client context.**
