Since Copilot made its way into Power Automate, I often hear the same question:

*"With AI, do we still need an expert?"*

And honestly, I get it. Copilot generates flows in natural language. It suggests actions, structures conditions, recommends connectors. It's impressive.

But building a flow is the easy part.

---

## What AI Does Very Well

Let's be honest: AI has changed the way I work, and that's a good thing.

- It generates a **first draft** of a flow in seconds
- It helps me **recall the syntax** of an expression I've forgotten
- It suggests **alternatives** when I'm looking for the right connector
- It **explains an error** without me having to dig through documentation

I use it every day. It's made me faster.

But faster at what, exactly? At the technical part. At implementation.

And implementation, in a Power Automate project, is rarely where the real stakes lie.

---

## What AI Can't Do

### 1. Understand What the Client Actually Wants

When a client tells me *"we want to automate our purchase orders"*, that's not a flow description. It's a starting point.

What needs to be untangled from there:

- Who approves? In what order? What if the approver is absent?
- What is a "purchase order" for them? An email? An Excel file? A scanned paper form?
- Which systems need to be updated in parallel?
- What happens if the order is rejected at step 3 out of 4?

AI doesn't ask these questions. It takes the description at face value and builds something that looks like it works. Something that doesn't match the reality on the ground.

> **AI starts from the need as it's expressed. An expert starts from the need as it's actually experienced.**

---

### 2. Navigate Environmental Constraints

Power Automate is an ecosystem. And that ecosystem has its rules, its limits, its traps.

| Constraint | What AI Ignores |
|---|---|
| **Licenses** | Which premium connector is activated? Who has a Power Automate per-user license? |
| **Governance** | Do DLP policies allow this connector in this environment? |
| **Platform limits** | How many actions per flow? What data volume is expected? |
| **Connectors** | Is the standard version enough, or is the premium connector needed? |
| **Environments** | Dev, test, prod: how to manage connections and variables across environments? |

A flow generated by Copilot can be technically correct and completely unusable in a given client context.

---

### 3. Anticipate What Will Break

Field experience can't be improvised. And after years of projects, I have a mental catalogue of "things that always break":

- Flows that fail silently with no error notification
- Conditions that don't cover edge cases
- Automations that work in testing and fail in production due to volume
- Approval timeouts that nobody had anticipated
- Flows that break after an unannounced Microsoft update

AI builds for the nominal case. It doesn't build for reality.

---

### 4. Support Change

A deployed flow that nobody uses is a failed project.

The technical part is only half the work. The other half is:

- Explaining to teams **why** their process is changing
- Training the functional administrators who will maintain the solution
- Managing resistance (*"we used to do it this way and it worked just fine"*)
- Adapting the solution when field feedback reveals unexpressed needs

AI delivers a solution. An expert delivers **adoption**.

---

### 5. Build Something That Lasts

Power Automate lives in a Microsoft environment that continuously evolves. What works today may need adjustment in six months.

What I put in place on every project:

- **Technical-functional documentation** that teams can revisit without me
- **Error handling mechanisms** and reporting to monitor incidents
- An architecture that anticipates **foreseeable business changes**
- **End-to-end testing** before each deployment

It's not just about building. It's about building to last.

---

## AI: My Tool, Not My Replacement

The real question isn't *"will AI replace experts?"* but *"what is valuable in an expert's work?"*

What has value is not knowing how to place a "Send an email" action after a "Create SharePoint item" action. Anyone can do that. Copilot too.

What has value is:

✅ **Translating a vague business need into a robust solution**
✅ **Knowing the platform's limits before hitting them**
✅ **Building what holds up over time**
✅ **Supporting teams through change**
✅ **Being accountable for the outcome, not just the delivery**

AI helps me go faster on the technical side. It frees up time to focus on what really matters: understanding the business, scoping the need, delivering a solution that works in real life.

> **It's not the tool that makes the expert. It's what you do with it.**
