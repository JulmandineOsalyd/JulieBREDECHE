> *"What's the difference between Copilot, a Copilot agent and Copilot Cowork?"*

In this article, I want to clarify things with a concrete case: how an HR manager handles the onboarding of 3 new hires.

---

## A concrete case to understand the use cases: HR onboarding

Monday morning, 9am. Sophie, HR manager in a company of 800 people, opens her calendar.
This week, three new hires are arriving: a salesperson, a project manager and a developer.

For each of them, she needs to **prepare a personalized onboarding file**: present the organization, identify the right contacts, recap the ongoing projects in the team they are joining, organize the first meetings with their managers.

Before Cowork, Copilot helped her occasionally: rewriting an email, summarizing a meeting. Useful, but most of the actions still had to be performed by HR.

### 9am: Sophie delegates

She formulates an objective to Copilot Cowork, just as she would to a team member, then heads to her meeting:

> *"For each of the three new hires joining this week, prepare a personalized onboarding file: organization, ongoing projects in their team, key people to meet, a 'who does what' guide tailored to their role. The job descriptions are in my SharePoint Recruitment 2026."*


### 9am to 11:30am: Cowork executes

Cowork builds a 7 step plan:

- Reading the job descriptions
- Identifying the target teams
- Scanning Teams channels to spot active projects and recent decisions
- Navigating the team SharePoints
- Identifying key contacts through emails and meetings
- Producing three Word files
- Dropping them in the HR SharePoint



⚠️ Key point: **Cowork strictly inherits the user's permissions.** If Sophie doesn't have access to a private Teams channel, Cowork doesn't either.

### 11:30am: Sophie reviews

Three files are waiting for her review. Cowork flags two points:

- *"I hesitated on the developer's target team: the job description mentions Data, but manager exchanges point more to Platform. Can you confirm?"*
- *"I don't have access to the private Project Phoenix channel, which is relevant for the project manager. Request access, or stick to public sources only?"*

An effective agent knows how to **acknowledge its limits and ask for help** rather than act without a safety net.

The result: 30 minutes reviewing the onboarding files instead of several hours of manual preparation.

## Which Copilot for which use case?

| | **M365 Copilot** | **Copilot Agents** | **Copilot Cowork** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What for** | Everyday tasks | Repetitive and specialized tasks | Heavy lifting and automation |
| **Duration** | A few seconds | Seconds to minutes | Minutes to several hours |
| **Analogy** | An assistant who answers in real time | An intern specialized on one task | An assistant you hand a file to |
| **Examples** | • Drafting an email<br/>• Finding information in a Teams chat | • Sorting expense reports by category<br/>• Extracting key info from a supplier invoice | • Preparing a client file before a meeting<br/>• Producing the monthly reporting (figures + comments + slides) |

## My conclusion

M365 Copilot, Agents, Cowork: these are not three versions of the same product, but **three levels of autonomy** we grant to AI. And the more we delegate, the more the groundwork matters.

Concretely, three topics will come up in nearly every Cowork engagement:

1. **Permission quality**. Cowork inherits user rights: any fuzziness in SharePoint permissions or private Teams channels becomes an operational risk.
2. **Use case scoping**. Who can use Cowork? For what? On which data? With what level of human validation?
3. **User training**. Formulating an objective, validating a plan, redirecting an agent: all new reflexes to acquire.
