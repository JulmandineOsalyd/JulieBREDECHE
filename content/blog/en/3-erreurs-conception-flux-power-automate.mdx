Someone hands me a flow to update. I open it, and I run into "Apply to each 4", "Compose 5", no error handling.

Here are three mistakes that come up too often, and my recommendations to avoid them.

---

## Mistake #1: Poorly named actions

`Apply to each 4`, `Compose 5`, `Condition 2`. What do these actions do? Nobody knows. Not even the person who built the flow three months earlier.

Once the flow gets complex, it's impossible to find your way around. And if a colleague has to take it over, it's even worse.

**Best practice: apply a shared naming rule across all your flows.**

- **Keep the original action name**: leave `Apply to each` or `Compose` so the action type stays obvious at a glance
- **Summarize what the action does** in two or three words max, using **camelCase** for the descriptive part: `documentFound`, `fileMoved`

Concretely, `Apply to each 4` becomes `Apply to each - documentFound`. You instantly understand what the loop is processing.

## Mistake #2: No error handling

An HTTP call fails. A piece of data is missing. The flow stops. Without warning.

Without error handling, you discover bugs after the fact. And it's often the end user who notices first.

**Best practice: use the "Run after" settings combined with an automatic notification action** (Teams, email, or both).

Concretely, you add a notification action right after the critical step. On that action, you configure **Run after** so it triggers in every failure case:

- ❌ &nbsp; **Is successful**: unchecked (otherwise you get notified on every successful run)
- ✅ &nbsp; **Has timed out**: checked
- ✅ &nbsp; **Is skipped**: checked
- ✅ &nbsp; **Has failed**: checked

<div className="prose-article70"><img src="/images/blog/PA-Erreur2.png" alt="Power Automate Run after configuration with error notification" /></div><br/>

The end user no longer sees the bug before you do. The flow fails, you get notified, and you can fix it before the incident escalates.

---

## Mistake #3: Too many nested conditions

If A, then B. Unless C. But only if D. That's not a flow anymore, it's an epic.

The flow becomes unreadable, and maintenance becomes nearly impossible.

**Best practice: break the complexity with three levers.**

- **Logical expressions**: replace three nested conditions with a single `if(and(...), ..., ...)` expression in a `Compose` action
- **The Switch action**: a Switch is far more readable than a cascade of `Condition` blocks
- **Splitting into multiple flows**: when the logic stays heavy, extract part of it into one or several child flows. [Trigger Conditions](/blog/trigger-conditions-power-automate) can also help!

For a per-file-type treatment, for example, a Switch on `documentType` with a Case for each value (`wordFile`, `excelFile`, `Default`) is a better fit than three stacked `If` conditions.

<div className="prose-article70"><img src="/images/blog/PA-Erreur3.png" alt="Switch documentType action with Case wordFile excelFile Default" /></div><br/>

> **If your flow looks like a Christmas tree with ten `If` branches, it needs to be rethought, not extended.**

---

## My takeaway

A well-designed flow is a flow you can understand at a glance.

Three observations that come up in most of the audits I run:

1. Naming actions takes a few seconds at design time, and saves hours of maintenance later.
2. Without automatic failure notifications, the end user ends up being your monitoring system.
3. A flow nobody can read is a flow nobody will dare to evolve.

Design the flow for whoever will pick it up six months from now. That person could very well be you.
