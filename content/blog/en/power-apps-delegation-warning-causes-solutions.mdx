The application works fine, data displays correctly during testing, and the project goes live.

A few weeks later, a user reports: *"Some of my data is missing."* Or worse: no one notices, and decisions are being made on incomplete data.

The root cause, in most cases: a **delegation warning** ignored from the very beginning of the project.

---

## The Delegation Warning in Power Apps

When Power Apps displays *"Delegation warning. The highlighted part of this formula may not work correctly on large data sets"*, this is not a theoretical notice.

It means **the formula cannot be processed by the data source**: records are retrieved locally, and the formula is applied client-side, on a capped volume.

On the client side, limits apply:

- **500 records** retrieved by default
- Configurable maximum: **2,000**
- Everything beyond that: **ignored**

> **If your data source contains 5,000 rows, Power Apps sees at most 2,000.**

When delegation works correctly, Power Apps sends the formula directly to the data source, which processes it server-side with no volume limit.

---

## Why Ignoring This Warning Is Risky

The real trap: **this is not a visible error**. The application works. It displays data. There is no obvious sign that records are missing.

Common symptoms:

- Only the most recent records appear
- A filter returns inconsistent results
- Results vary between users

⚠️ **Beyond 2,000 rows in the data source, the application returns incomplete results — with no error message.**

---

## The Most Common Causes

### 1. Non-delegable functions

Some functions cannot be delegated to the data source:

- `Search()` (for most connectors)
- `CountIf()`
- `If()` inside a `Filter()`
- The `in` operator

Problematic example: `Filter(Employees, "Julie" in Name)`

This filter forces Power Apps to retrieve all records before filtering.

### 2. SharePoint used as an application database

SharePoint has stricter delegation limits than Dataverse or SQL. Many operations are not supported: `StartsWith()` on certain column types, sorting on calculated columns...

SharePoint is designed to store documents and reasonably sized lists — not for complex filters on thousands of rows.

### 3. The Search() function on large datasets

`Search()` is one of the most commonly used functions, and one of the most problematic on large datasets.

`Search(Employees, TextInput1.Text, "Name")`

This function is not delegable on most connectors. It systematically triggers a warning as soon as the volume exceeds the 2,000-record limit.

---

## 3 Workarounds, From Simplest to Most Structural

### Solution 1 • Replace Search() with Filter() and StartsWith()

Instead of: `Search(Employees, TextInput1.Text, "Name")`

Use: `Filter(Employees, StartsWith(Name, TextInput1.Text))`

⚠️ `StartsWith()` is delegable in Dataverse, but only on single-line text columns in SharePoint.

### Solution 2 • Filter at the source, not in Power Apps

The goal: avoid letting Power Apps handle filtering.

- **Dataverse**: create filtered views and expose them to the application
- **SharePoint**: index the columns used in filters, create pre-filtered views

Power Apps should consume already-optimized data, not sort through it.

### Solution 3 • Use collections with a delegable filter upstream

`ClearCollect()` alone solves nothing: the collection is still subject to the 2,000-record limit at load time.

The right approach: **pre-filter server-side before loading the collection.**

`ClearCollect(colData, Filter(Employees, Status = "Active"))`

The filter is executed by the server. The collection only loads the matching records, with no limit applied. All formulas applied to the collection afterwards work without restriction.

Three conditions for this to work:

- The filter targets an **indexed column**
- The operator is delegable (`=`, `<`, `>`, `StartsWith` depending on the source)
- The column is not calculated

> **This is the only collection-based approach that does not truncate data.**

## How to Identify What Is Delegable

Power Apps includes visual indicators directly in the formula editor:

- **Single blue underline**: delegable
- **Double blue underline**: partially delegable
- **Warning icon**: not delegable

Two additional habits to adopt:

- **Hover over the warning**: Power Apps identifies which part of the formula is causing the issue
- **Test with real data volumes**: 50 test records reveal nothing. 3,000 records do.

Microsoft documentation lists delegable functions connector by connector. Consult it before writing a filter: [Power Apps delegable functions and operations for SharePoint](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/power-apps/maker/canvas-apps/connections/connection-sharepoint-online#power-apps-delegable-functions-and-operations-for-sharepoint).

---

## My Conclusion

A delegation warning is not a message to dismiss. It is Power Apps signaling that the application may not scale.

Three observations I consistently encounter in the field:

1. **Delegation warnings ignored during development become bugs in production**: test datasets are too small to surface the problem. Testing with real data volumes from the start is essential.
2. **Data source selection is an architectural decision**: SharePoint for a few hundred rows, Dataverse once the volume grows.
3. **Delegation is not a Power Apps bug**: it is a design constraint. Working around it without understanding it only moves the problem — it does not solve it.
