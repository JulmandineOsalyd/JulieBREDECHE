We've all seen it. The arrival of AI in the workplace almost always follows the same adoption curve. It's the classic technology innovation cycle: a rocket start, followed by a frustrating radio silence.

As a consultant, I too often see organizations invest heavily in licenses only to find, three months later, that users have gone back to their "old ways."

**Why this burnout? And more importantly, how do you turn these new technologies into a genuine productivity lever?**

---

## Analyzing the Adoption Curve

### Phase 1: The Boom

At launch, excitement runs high. License activation triggers immediate curiosity. People test meeting summaries in Teams ("Amazing, no more note-taking!"), rewrite a couple of emails in Outlook.

This is the _quick wins_ phase. The tool feels magical.

![Copilot Adoption Curve — Phase 1](/images/blog/CourbeAdoptionCopilotPart1.png)

### Phase 2: The Burnout

Enthusiasm fades as limitations appear. Copilot can't find the right documents, generates imprecise answers due to lack of context, or produces inaccurate information.

Trust erodes. Teams disengage.

Three main obstacles explain this drop-off:

1. **Data silos**: the tool can't access the right information
2. **Lack of prompting skills**: teams don't know how to phrase their requests effectively
3. **Return to old habits**: some tasks feel faster to do manually

> *This is where the return on your investment is decided.*

## My 5-Step Proposal for a Sustainable Rollout

<div className="prose-articlemax"><img src="/images/blog/Sans-titre-3.png" alt="The 5 steps of a sustainable Copilot rollout" /></div>

### Step 1: Involve Top Management

Adoption can't rest solely on IT. If your executive team doesn't publicly use Copilot, no one will. It's not just about allocating a budget, but about embodying the change.

A leader who says: _"Copilot helped me prepare this briefing in 10 minutes instead of 2 hours, I could focus on the analysis"_ demonstrates concrete time savings.

### Step 2: Technical Prerequisites and Change Management

**Technical side:** secure data governance before deployment. If your SharePoint permissions are too broad, Copilot will surface confidential information to people who shouldn't see it.

**Human side:** train pilot users on prompting fundamentals and best practices for validating responses.

🔗 Link to my article: [\[Copilot A to Z\] - Mastering the basics of prompting](https://juliebredeche.pro/blog/bases-prompting-copilot)

### Step 3: A Targeted Pilot Phase

Start with a small, diverse group: **30 licenses maximum, 100% supported**.

- Include profiles from different departments
- Identify **AI Champions** by business area, capable of translating technical capabilities into concrete benefits for their team
- Document the use cases that actually work

### Step 4: Let Usage Create the Need

Don't try to impose rigid processes. Let teams experiment around their real problems.

> **Field feedback**: I recently deployed a declarative agent specifically dedicated to onboarding for a client.
Connected to their HR SharePoint site, it answers new hire questions ("Where do I find the health insurance info?", "How do I request time off?").
Adoption was immediate because the tool solved a concrete problem: the overload of repetitive questions to HR.

This type of use case, born from real needs, generates organic, lasting adoption.

### Step 5: Don't Look for Immediate ROI

This is the fatal mistake: trying to calculate time saved to the minute from day one. Generative AI requires a learning curve.

Don't measure **"time saved"** immediately, but **"quality gained"**. Financial ROI will follow naturally once the tool becomes a natural extension of employee skills.

---

## Recommended Timeline for a Successful Rollout

1. **Data governance first**: secure SharePoint permissions, tidy the house before inviting AI in
2. **Identify motivated business areas** with an executive sponsor
3. **Restricted pilot** with intensive support
4. **Activate licenses alongside training**, not before, not long after
5. **Hands-on workshops** to co-build impactful use cases
6. **Scale successes** to inspire broader adoption

---

## Conclusion

Post-launch burnout is not inevitable. It is the symptom of a deployment that was too fast, without support, on a fragile technical foundation.

Organizations that succeed with Copilot adoption are those that invest as much in **data governance** and **change management** as in the licenses themselves.
